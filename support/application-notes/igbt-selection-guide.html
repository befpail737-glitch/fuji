How to Select the Right IGBT for Your Application

Complete guide on selecting the right IGBT for your power electronics application

January 20, 2025 By LiTong Technical Team Application Note

Selecting the right IGBT is critical for achieving optimal performance, efficiency, and reliability in your power electronics application. This guide provides a comprehensive approach to IGBT selection, considering electrical, thermal, and application-specific requirements.

Introduction

Insulated Gate Bipolar Transistors (IGBTs) are critical components in power electronics applications, combining the high input impedance of MOSFETs with the low conduction losses of bipolar transistors. Selecting the right IGBT for your application involves considering multiple parameters including voltage rating, current capability, switching characteristics, and thermal performance.

Key Selection Criteria

1. Voltage Rating

The voltage rating of an IGBT should be selected based on the maximum voltage it will see in your application. As a general rule, the IGBT's voltage rating should be at least 1.5 times the maximum operating voltage to provide adequate safety margin.

For example, in a 400V AC system (which has a peak voltage of approximately 565V), an IGBT with a voltage rating of at least 1200V is recommended to account for switching transients and safety margins.

2. Current Rating

The current rating must accommodate the maximum continuous and peak currents in your application. Consider both DC and AC current components, as well as the effects of conduction losses on junction temperature.

Derating factors should be applied based on operating conditions:

  • Temperature: IGBTs must be derated at higher temperatures
  • Switching frequency: Higher frequencies increase switching losses
  • Cooling method: Active cooling allows higher current ratings

3. Switching Frequency

The switching frequency directly affects both switching and conduction losses. At higher frequencies, switching losses dominate, so IGBTs with lower gate charge (Qg) and Miller charge (Qgc) are preferred. At lower frequencies, conduction losses dominate, so IGBTs with lower VCE(sat) are preferred.

Typical frequency ranges:

  • Low frequency: <2kHz, VCE(sat) optimized devices
  • Medium frequency: 2-10kHz, balanced loss devices
  • High frequency: >10kHz, specialized high-frequency devices

4. Switching Characteristics

Soft switching characteristics are important for reducing EMI and voltage stress. The turn-on and turn-off characteristics can often be adjusted through gate resistance, but the intrinsic characteristics of the IGBT are important for performance.

IGBT Generation Comparison

Fuji Electric's IGBT technology has evolved through multiple generations, each offering improvements in efficiency and performance:

6th Generation

The 6th Generation IGBTs offer good balance of switching and conduction losses, suitable for industrial motor drives and UPS applications. These devices provide reliable performance across a wide range of applications.

7th Generation (X-Series)

The 7th Generation X-Series IGBTs provide approximately 20% lower switching losses and 10% lower conduction losses compared to the 6th Generation. These are ideal for applications where efficiency is critical, particularly at switching frequencies above 10kHz.

PrimePACK™ Series

For high-power applications, PrimePACK™ IGBTs offer superior current density and thermal performance in a compact package, ideal for automotive and industrial applications.

Package Selection

The package choice affects both thermal performance and system integration:

  • Discrete packages (TO-247, TO-220): Good for prototyping and low-volume applications
  • Industrial modules (6-pack, 2-pack): Better thermal performance and EMI characteristics
  • Press-fit packages: Simplified assembly and improved thermal cycling performance

Thermal Considerations

Proper thermal design is critical for reliable IGBT operation:

  • Calculate total power losses (switching + conduction + gate)
  • Determine required thermal resistance to maintain safe junction temperatures
  • Consider thermal cycling and its impact on long-term reliability
  • Account for derating at elevated temperatures

Protection Requirements

IGBTs require protection against:

  • Over-current: Use current sensing and fast turn-off
  • Over-voltage: Implement clamping circuits for inductive loads
  • Over-temperature: Monitor case temperature for thermal protection
  • Desaturation: Detect fault conditions and implement safe turn-off

Design Example

For a 10kW motor drive powered by 400V AC supply operating at 15kHz switching frequency:

  1. Voltage rating: 1200V minimum to provide safety margin
  2. Current rating: Calculate RMS current: 10kW / (400V * √3 * 0.95 efficiency) = 15.2 Arms
  3. IGBT selection: 2MBI100XAA-50 (7th Generation X-Series) offers optimal balance of losses at 15kHz
  4. Thermal design: Calculate losses (approx. 150W) to size heatsink appropriately

Testing and Validation

After IGBT selection, validate the design through:

  • Loss calculation verification
  • Thermal testing under worst-case conditions
  • EMI/EMC compliance testing
  • Accelerated life testing for reliability validation

Conclusion

Selecting the right IGBT requires a balance of multiple parameters including voltage rating, current capability, switching characteristics, and thermal performance. By following a systematic approach and considering all operational conditions, you can select an IGBT that provides optimal performance, efficiency, and reliability for your application.

When in doubt, consult with our application engineering team for specific guidance tailored to your particular application requirements.
